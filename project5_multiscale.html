Multiscale Simulation

DFT & Molecular Dynamics

Overview

This project focuses on elucidating complex behaviors and mechanisms within MOFs, polymers, catalysts, and battery systems through density functional theory (DFT) and molecular dynamics (MD) simulations. By employing multiscale computational approaches, we can understand material properties at the atomic level and predict their macroscopic behavior.

DFT Simulations: Catalyst Surface Reactivity

Cobalt-Doped Ceria Sensitizer Effects on Metal Oxide Nanofibers

To support the experimental findings and rationalize the underlying mechanism behind the enhanced sensing properties in Co–CeO₂@SnO₂, we conducted DFT calculations to investigate the adsorption energies and conducted Bader charge analyses.

Computational Methodology

DFT calculations were performed using Vienna Ab initio Simulation Program (VASP) software version 5.4.1. The Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE) exchange–correlation functional and projector-augmented wave (PAW) pseudopotentials were utilized in the calculations.

  • PBE-D3 method of Grimme was used to account for van der Waals interactions
  • Dipole corrections were implemented to alleviate artificial potential gradients
  • CeO₂ surface cleaved to expose the (111) facet
  • 9 atomic layers model with a 20 Å vacuum gap perpendicular to the surface
  • Co–CeO₂ surface obtained by substituting one Co atom in the CeO₂ surface

Results & Insights

Adsorption energies of C₅H₈ on both CeO₂ and Co–CeO₂ surfaces were calculated for different possible configurations. The results revealed that the adsorption energy of C₅H₈ on Co–CeO₂ (−83.5 kJ mol⁻¹) was higher compared to that of CeO₂ (−67.6 kJ mol⁻¹), indicating more favorable adsorption of the target analyte on the Co–CeO₂ surface.

Key Finding: The charge density difference plot, along with the Bader charge analysis, indicates an increase in the absolute value of charge transfer from 0.0263 e for CeO₂ to 0.0401 e for Co–CeO₂. A larger magnitude of charge transfer in Co-doped CeO₂ would indicate a significant reinforcement in the C₅H₈ adsorption and activation behavior.

Molecular Dynamics: Crosslinking Behavior

Investigating Crosslinker Chain Length Effects

We performed molecular dynamics (MD) simulations to investigate the effect of crosslinker chain length on the crosslinking behavior. In the model, two parallel planes positioned along the top and bottom represented the surfaces of two different perovskite nanocrystals (PeNCs), with only surface-bound ligands included to simplify the system.

Simulation Approach

To mimic the bound state, the terminal oxygen atom of each ligand was fixed in space. Dithiol crosslinkers were randomly distributed in the space between the two surfaces. To model bond formation, we assumed that an irreversible bond would form during the MD simulation whenever a sulfur atom from a crosslinker approached within a defined cutoff distance of a C═C bond in a surface ligand.

This was implemented using a distance-based bond formation algorithm, which has been previously applied to simulate self-assembly processes. After the simulations, we classified the total crosslinking (ntotal) into two types:

  • Interparticle crosslinking (external, next) - contributes to solubility change
  • Internal crosslinking (nint) - does not contribute to solubility change
Key Results: HDT, with a sulfur-to-sulfur distance of 0.95 nm, showed a low crosslinking efficiency of 0.167. In contrast, longer-chain crosslinkers such as ODT (1.19 nm) and DDT (1.23 nm) exhibited higher crosslinking efficiencies of 0.357 and 0.64, respectively.

Monte Carlo Simulations: MOF Adsorption

Hydrogen-Bonding Networks in Flexible MOFs

To further confirm the relationship between pore size and imidazole-mediated hydrogen-bonding networks, molecular simulations were used to populate the imidazole and water molecules inside the pores of Im@MIL-88B-SB and Im@MIL-88B-LB.

Grand Canonical Monte Carlo (GCMC) Simulations

The amount of introduced imidazole molecules through the Grand Canonical Monte Carlo simulation is 3.52 and 9.81 per Fe clusters for Im@MIL-88B-SB and Im@MIL-88B-LB, respectively.

The imidazole molecules in Im@MIL-88B-SB are found near the vicinity of the metal clusters while the relatively empty space is observed around the linker, forming isolated clusters due to the small pore size. However, in Im@MIL-88B-LB, imidazole is evenly distributed within the pore, with the continuous hydrogen-bonding networks being formed.

NVT Simulations: Water-Imidazole Interactions

Next, the water–imidazole adsorption behaviors in Im@MIL-88B-SB and Im@MIL-88B-LB were obtained through the NVT simulations, in which the number of adsorbed water molecules was set to be in accordance with the experimental results.

Key Finding: Imidazole and water molecules are continuously mixed in Im@MIL-88B-LB, contributing to efficient hydrogen-bonding networks. The enhanced coordination numbers of atom pairs between different chemical constituents in Im@MIL-88B-LB indicate the mixed and continuous configurations of proton carriers. The hydrogen-bonding numbers for each atom of proton carriers in Im@MIL-88B-LB are increased to more than one, contributing to the formation of continuous hydrogen-bonding networks.
